Where Dastak Begins

I did not arrive at this work through a textbook. I arrived through a journey full of experiences

When I spearheaded the organization of our school's MUN conference in collaboration with CRY NGO fundraising for a child's education and meeting kids who wanted to learn, I went in expecting to feel moved. I did not expect to feel challenged.

What I found was curiosity restlessness, almost impatient. These children wanted to learn. They asked questions faster than we could answer them. They were excited in a way that made me question every time I had taken my own education for granted.

But alongside that curiosity, I realised that every opportunity, every policy, every scheme designed for them had been built entirely without them. And by people who had never sat in that room. The result was initiatives that existed on paper and evaporated on the ground.

Project "Dastak" is my answer to that room. Named after the Hindi word for a knock on the door, it is built on one belief that civic literacy is not a privilege for law students. It is a right. And a child, a girl, who learns to read a policy document is not just educated but is finally inside the room of decision-making.

Why This Matters

Policy shapes every child's life.
Yet no one teaches them to read it.

Children, particularly girls, from underfunded communities navigate a world entirely structured by policy. Almost none have ever been taught to decode it.

The Programme : August to December 2026

Four sessions across four months
Result: One playbook that outlasts us.

Dastak, for me, is not a one-off event. It is a structured programme that leaves behind something permanent, childres, girls who know how to knock on doors they thought were closed, and a guide for others to follow.

01

August, 2026

Ground & Connect

Partner with underfunded government schools and community centres in South Delhi. Build relationships with students aged 13–17. Map their lived encounter with policy including what schemes do they know, what decisions affect them daily, what questions they have never been allowed to ask.

02

September, 2026

Decode

Sessions 1 & 2. Using real government documents like education budgets, safety laws, welfare schemes translated into plain language, students learn anout questions such as "What is a policy?" or "How does a law affect my daily life?" They annotate, question, and interpret documents that govern their own world.

03

October, 2026

Amplify

Sessions 3 & 4. Students practise civic participation by writing petitions, drafting letters to local representatives, speaking at student councils. A guest speaker working in civic life in Delhi would join us to show them what the room looks like from the inside.

04

Nov-Dec 2026

Document & Release

Students co-author a Policy Brief on an issue they identify themselves and submit it to their school's student council or administration. The Dastak Playbook, compiling every session plan and facilitation note, is published open-source for any campus in India to replicate.

The "Dastak" Playbook

What we build will outlast a semester.

The most important thing Dastak will produce is not the sessions. It is the Playbook which would be a complete, open-source facilitation guide that any undergraduate student, anywhere in India, can pick up and run at their own school.

No budget required, no prior expertise needed. Just four sessions to achieve civic literacy.

Every module is designed to feel less like a class and more like a conversation because the goal was never to lecture children, girls, our future about policy. It was to sit with them, decode it together, and hand them the language to demand what is already theirs.

The Playbook will include full session plans, facilitation scripts, real government documents decoded into plain language, petition and letter templates, a Policy Brief template co-designed with participants, and a complete replication guide for other campuses.

Who can use the Playbook?

- Any undergraduate student at any Indian university

- NGOs and community organisations working with girls

- School teachers and counsellors in government schools

- Student councils and social impact clubs

Dastak advances four of the UN's Sustainable Development Goals because we believe fixing one inequality always moves the others.

5 Gender Equality

SDG 5.5: ensuring women's full participation in political and civic life. Dastak makes this concrete and ground-level.

10 Reduced Inequalities

Policy language has always been a gate. Dastak tries to removes the gate for children, who needed it dismantled the most.

16 Strong Institutions

SDG 16.7: inclusive, participatory decision-making. Every Policy Brief a child, a girl, co-authors is a small act of institutional inclusion.

UN Academic Impact Principle Principle 2: Capacity Building via Access to Education

Civic literacy is not a privilege for law students. It is a right. Dastak ensures that access to education i.e. a real, usable, power-shifting education, reaches the children who need it most and receive it least.

The Vision

"A girl who learns that her voice is policy-relevant doesn't just knock. She opens the door."

Dastak would begin in one classroom in South Delhi. But the Playbook is designed from day 1 to travel to every campus, every state and to every girl who has been told that policy is not for her.

We do not need scale to start. We need one room, four sessions, and a group of children who were waiting for someone to ask them what they thought.
